ABOUT THE BRAND

Who we are.
Why it matters.

The Upskilling Labs is a citizen R&D community — not a course, not a platform. It's a working lab where professionals build real skills by doing real work with real people. The model is simple: show up, find a pod, work on something that matters.

Launched in January 2026 at the DC Public Library, over 300 people have come through programs, platforms, and mentorship. The format is replicable — designed so any civic community across the US can adopt it.

When you create materials under this brand, you're representing that. Every flyer, every post, every slide is an extension of a community that takes the work seriously. The rules in this guide protect that identity. Use them.

How to describe TUL in one sentence

"The Upskilling Labs is a citizen R&D community that helps professionals build hands-on AI skills through peer-powered cohorts — rooted in local libraries."

CONTENT LANGUAGE

Voice & Tone

Warm, plain, human. Written by someone who knows what they're talking about and believes the reader is capable of more than they think. Not corporate, not winking.

Second-person "you"
"You'll work in pods" — not "Participants work in pods." Speak directly to the reader. No royal "we."
Specific nouns
"Your local library," "12-week build cycle," "your pod." Not "community" or "platform."
Real verbs
Iterating, shipping, building, navigating. Never "unlock," "leverage," "revolutionize," or "empower."
Declarative imperatives
Short stacked lines at the top of anything. "Grow. Build. Launch." Three lines, parallel, period at the end.
No hype or emoji
No exclamation points in marketing copy. No emoji. The brand earns excitement through specificity, not punctuation.
Plain present tense
"We run a working lab." Not "We will empower you to unlock your potential." Plain, declarative, confident.
Signature patterns — use these verbatim
Hero
"Build real skills. Through real work. With real people." — last line in teal #0094A0
Subhead
"A place to learn by doing, alongside people who take the work seriously."
CTA
"Join The Upskilling Labs" or "Join The Labs" — never "Sign up" or "Get started"
Footer
"Hands-On Learning  ·  Peer-Driven Community  ·  Local Expert Network"
Words we never use
seamless powerful supercharge revolutionize amazing awesome unlock leverage empower game-changing cutting-edge innovative disruptive transformative
VISUAL LANGUAGE

Backgrounds & Textures

Three dark background types — all combined with an SVG grain noise overlay. Light sections use off-white #F6F4EF or white.

Never two consecutive dark sections without a light break in between.

MODE 1
Ink → Navy
Quiet mode
Footers, editorial dark sections, stat/breath surfaces. The most common dark background.
linear-gradient(180deg, #00141B, #03232A)
MODE 2
Navy → Teal
Hero / Pillars mode
Hero sections, the Pillars strip, testimonials. Teal radiates from bottom or corners — never from the center.
linear-gradient(180deg, #03232A, #007882)
MODE 3
Diagonal Sweep
Dynamic mode
Social squares, event posters, closing CTAs. Soft radial teal from a corner over a 135° linear. No hard edges.
135° linear + radial teal from corner
Radial rule — the most important spatial constraint

Teal radiates outward from corners and edges. The logo, orb, and headlines always sit over a dark navy center. Never place a teal blob in the middle of a composition. Dark ink at center → teal at edges is the rule.

REFERENCE

Do's & Don'ts

The most common mistakes and their correct alternatives.

✓
Do
Use Geologica at every size — display, body, caption, eyebrow
Place the logo on dark navy only, with generous clear space
Add SVG grain overlay to every dark or gradient surface
Let teal radiate outward from corners — never from the center
Write second-person "you," plain present tense, specific nouns
Break between dark and light sections — never stack two dark
Use real typographic glyphs — em-dash (—) en-dash (–) middot (·)
✗
Don't
Use any typeface other than Geologica — no Inter, no Arial
Place the logo on teal, red, white, or any light background
Center a teal gradient blob behind the logo or headline
Use gradient backgrounds inside cards, or left-border accent strips
Use emoji, exclamation points, or the forbidden word list
Use photos with horizon lines, stairs, blinds, or warm tones
Drop shadows on text, UI elements, or the logo mark itself
